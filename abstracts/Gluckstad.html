In this talk we will discuss a larger ongoing project (based on the core theory introduced in an upcoming paper by Duquesne and Winkel \([4]\)), developing the theory of mass erasure. The main difficulty this theory addresses is introducing a natural framework in which one may study convergence of finitely \([4]\) / infinitely \([5a]\) measured Polish \(\mathbb{R}\)-trees which are not locally compact. Such \(\mathbb{R}\)-trees may be thought of as containing large ‘hubs’, either in the form of infinite-degree branch points or accumulations of branch points, in which infinitely many of the subtrees featured are taller than some fixed size, say \(\varepsilon > 0\).

Mass erasure provides a technique for trimming such \(\mathbb{R}\)-trees in a similar way to the trimming or leaf-length erasure known from e.g., \([8]\) or \([3]\), but where subtrees are erased according to their mass rather than their height with respect to some erasure parameter \(h > 0\) (controlling how much mass is erased, with the tree being fully recovered as \(h \searrow 0\)). This allows for the extraction of simpler discrete subtrees, which may easily be studied using the classical Gromov-type topologies. Considering mass erasure across multiple parameters \(h > 0\) tending to zero, we may construct new topologies of (weak/vague) convergence in the sense of mass erasure.
We will in particular focus on how the theory of mass erasure may be applied to obtain invariance principles for Galton-Watson forests in a broader setting than what has been done in existing literature \([2,3]\). Namely, we will look at how to construct Lévy forests when the so-called Grey’s condition fails, using convergence in the sense of mass erasure, thus providing in full generality an analogous result to \([6]\) in the setting of random trees. If time permits, we may also discuss some newer ongoing work aiming to characterize finitely measured Polish \(\mathbb{R}\)-trees using coding-functions, in a similar manner to how height functions were used to encode and study the convergence of compact \(\mathbb{R}\)-trees in \([2]\).
